The Art of Crafting Sustainability: An EthnoGRAPHIC Study through Observational Sketching

Field Study Participant Information Sheet

A web version of this document can be found at the link: Online Participant Information Sheet

Hello, if you are reading this document is because you are being invited to take part in the ethnoGRAPHIC study The Art of Crafting Sustainability. In this document you will find information about this study and its overarching PhD research: what are the goals, how the study is conducted, and what taking part does and does not involve. Please read it carefully.

In the final section you can find further resources about the PhD and contact details for any clarification, question, or doubt.

Brief summary

The Art of Crafting Sustainability is a visual and artistic ethnographic study that investigates how maker initiatives in Devon and the Southwest are promoting and building sustainability behaviours and ecological cultures.
The study is part of the PhD project Sustainability in the Making: Craft Programmes for Eco-Social Change, a doctoral research that explores how craft and making contribute to generate and promote culture(s) of sustainability. This PhD is a doctoral training partnership by Kingston University - Kingston School of Art (academic partner) and the Crafts Council UK (non-academic partner), and it is sponsored by the Arts and Humanities Research Council (AHRC) Techne. This study is conducted by Alessandra (Alex) Fasoli, author of this document and PhD candidate in charge of the project.

About this PhD

This PhD focuses on how cultures of sustainability can be generated and promoted through craft and maker programmes.
Cultures of sustainability are collective systems of knowledge and customs rooted in environmental and ecological values, which allow individuals and communities to adopt more environmentally-friendly behaviours, lifestyles, and norms. Many studies on sustainable craft focus on the way makers incorporate sustainability in their creative process: for example how they use recycled or upcycled materials, create artefacts using local resources, or make objects with specific sustainable purposes.
This PhD, however, looks at sustainable craft from a different perspective, which sees craft and making as social activities through which people share, transform, and create cultural values, like preserving traditions, building local identities, sharing worldviews, creating a sense of place or shaping collective histories. Combining this definition of craft as a cultural tool and the concept of cultures of sustainability, this PhD asks if and how craft can be a vehicle to build sustainability cultures and aims to answer this question by studying existing craft programmes that promote specific environmental, ecological, and sustainable approaches through making.

What is the Art of Crafting Sustainability study about?

The Art of Crafting Sustainability is an ethnographic study based on participant observation, that uses observational sketching as its primary tool for gathering data. Observational sketching is a research method in which the researcher observing a groups uses drawing and sketches as its primary form of notetaking and data management. Alex, the researcher conducting the study, will join your event as an observant researcher and will draw on a sketchbook what she is observing.

What drawings will she make?

During her observation, Alex will be producing a variety of sketches that can be focused on a particular individual (figure 1, figure 2 left, figure 3 left); on a group (figure 2 right); and/or on a particular interaction (figure 3 right).

As the idea is to graphically describe the dynamics and relationships happening throughout the whole event, onsite drawings can be simple pencil or ink line sketches (figure 4) with colour added later (figure 1, 2, and 3). Alex might also add written notes and quotes to her drawings (figure 1).

What‚Äôs involved in taking part in the study?

The objective of this study is to understand which cultural dynamics are generated during maker- and craft-led events and to observe how engaging with sustainable making allows people to reflect and learn about the environment, the climate crisis, consumerism, and production. Participants in these events should carry on their activities as normal while Alex is observing them. She will interfere as little as possible, not to alter spontaneous behaviours with her own perspective.

Will there also be interviews?

Interviews are not part of this study, but conversations between participants and the researcher might happen. Alex might engage in conversations or ask specific questions after the observational work is concluded. Spontaneous conversations during the observational sketching are welcome, but the possible use of these conversations in the research study will be discussed and negotiated with participants on a case-by-case basis. However, Alex will arrange a small conversation before and after the event with workshop/event leaders, as they can give more insights of the activity (objectives, goals, impressions, comparisons with other iterations) and help contextualise it. These conversations will be recorded and used in the study following a previous agreement.

Are there other initiatives that will take part in the research?

The research focuses on sustainable craft programmes in Devon and the Southwest. This study will be carried in several craft events and workshops in the area that have a sustainability approach.

You agree for me to join you during your event/workshop. Can participants opt out?

Participation is voluntary. If someone participating in your event does not want to be involved in the study, the person will not be observed, meaning that there will be no sketches/drawings of them nor the researcher will collect any data about them. Participants who initially agreed to take part can opt out in any moment, meaning that they can withdraw their participation in the middle, at the end, or up to 20 days after the event; any sketches and drawings in which they could be identified will not be used; and any personal data concerning them will be destroyed or deleted.
If you are a workshop/event leader and want to withdraw from the study, this decision must be communicated before the event takes place. If Alex has already participated in the event, you can contact her within 20 days to anonymise the case study, meaning the name of the workshop/event and other identifiable data (comprised identifiable data of the leader)will be removed from the dataset. You can find more information on anonymity and confidentiality in the ‚ÄòData‚Äô section of this document. Group sketches and drawing or stylised sketches (figure 5) with no quotes or references to specific participants are conceptual, therefore Alex reserves the right to use them if she deems them valuable for the study. This applies to all conceptual sketches whose primary function is a general representation of a collective occurrence.

Will Alex take photos, videos, or recording during the event?

Previous agreement with the event leader(s) and participants, Alex might take contextual photos of her sketchbook in the field (figure 4). She might take pictures of herself during the act of drawing (or ask someone to). These are documentation photos that are meant to show the methodology in action. This study does not comprise other types of documentary visuals (portrait photos, videos) beyond drawings. Previous consent by the participant, Alex might record casual conversations between participants, and between them and her. The audio file will accompany the drawings in the research analysis and showcase.

What data will be collected?

Sensitive data collected vary depending on your role in the research:

If you are an event leader or organiser:

In this case, the necessary data collected about you are:

  • full name
  • profession / role in the organisation
  • background/education
  • ethnicity
  • age
If you are a participant in the event the researcher is joining:

In this case, the following data might be collected:

  • name (Optional: you can choose to stay anonymous)
  • age
  • ethnicity
  • gender
  • background/education
  • profession

How are your data processed?

All sensitive data are treated as confidential and will used only for the purpose of the research. Data collected in this study will be processed according to the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018.

What will happen to the drawings, notes, and audio files and the data generated by this study?

Drawings, notes, and audio files produced are part of an online collection/showcase that aims to illustrate the journey and findings of the PhD. Sketches and drawing can be presented as standalone pictures or paired with quotes through audio files and texts. The online showcase is built along the study, and its latest release can be found at https://sustainabilityinthemaking.com.
There you can find more information about the online showcase as a research tool for this project.

As part of a PhD, data and information generated by this study will analysed and discussed in the final dissertation, the primary output of the research. Datasets that will accompany the final dissertation will be aggregated data containing only the relevant information used in the research. Sensitive and confidential data will not be included in the dataset.

Finally, drawings, audio files, and data analysis can be presented in conferences and public talks, in person and online, in the form of digital presentations, installations, or videos. They can be part of exhibitions in galleries and public venues, and they can be published and printed in academic and non-academic manuscripts, in the form of scientific articles, essays, blogs, or books.
