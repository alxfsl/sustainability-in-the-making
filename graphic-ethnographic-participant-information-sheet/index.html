Brief summary

The Art of Crafting Sustainability is a visual and artistic ethnographic study that investigates how maker initiatives in Devon and the Southwest are promoting and building sustainability behaviours and ecological cultures.

The study is part of the PhD project Sustainability in the Making: Craft Programmes for Socio-Ecological Change, a doctoral research that explores how craft and making contribute to generate and promote culture(s) of sustainability.

This PhD is a doctoral training partnership by Kingston University - Kingston School of Art (academic partner) and the Crafts Council UK (non-academic partner), and it is sponsored by the Arts and Humanities Research Council (AHRC) Techne. This study is conducted by Alessandra (Alex) Fasoli, author of this document and PhD candidate in charge of the project.

About this PhD

This PhD focuses on how cultures of sustainability can be generated and promoted through craft and maker programmes.
Cultures of sustainability are collective systems of knowledge and customs rooted in environmental and ecological values, which allow individuals and communities to adopt more environmentally-friendly behaviours, lifestyles, and norms.
Many studies on *sustainable craft* focus on the way makers incorporate sustainability in their creative process: for example how they use recycled or upcycled materials, create artefacts using local resources, or make objects with specific sustainable purposes.
This PhD, however, looks at sustainable craft from a different perspective, which sees craft and making as social activities through which people share, transform, and create cultural values, like preserving traditions, building local identities, sharing worldviews, creating a sense of place, or shaping collective histories. Combining this definition of craft as a cultural tool and the concept of cultures of sustainability, this PhD asks in which ways craft contributes to build sustainability cultures and aims to answer this question by studying existing craft programmes that promote specific environmental, ecological, and sustainable approaches through making. This PhD focuses on craft initiatives in Devon and Southwest England.

What is the study The Art of Crafting Sustainability about?

The Art of Crafting Sustainability is an ethnographic study based on participant observation, that uses observational sketching as its primary tool for gathering data. Observational sketching is a research method in which the researcher observing a specific group or phenomenon uses drawing and sketches as its primary form of notetaking and data gathering.
I, Alex, the researcher conducting the study, will join your event as an observant researcher and will draw on a sketchbook what I observe.

What drawings will I make?

During my observation, I will be producing a variety of sketches that can be focused on a particular individual (figure 1, figure 2 left, figure 3 left); on a group (figure 2 right); and/or on a particular interaction (figure 3 right).
As the idea is to graphically describe the dynamics and relationships happening throughout the whole event, on-site drawings can be simple pencil or ink line sketches (figure 4) with colour added later (figure 1, 2, and 3). I might add written notes and quotes (figure 1).

What‚Äôs involved in taking part in the study?

The objective of this study is to understand which cultural dynamics are generated during maker- and craft-led events and to observe how engaging with sustainable making allows people to reflect and learn about the environment, the climate crisis, consumerism, and production. Participants in these events should carry on their activities as normal while I am observing them. I will interfere as little as possible, not to alter spontaneous behaviours with my own perspective.

Will there also be interviews?

Interviews are not part of this study, but conversations between participants and the researcher might happen. I might engage in conversations or ask specific questions after the observational work is concluded. Spontaneous conversations during the observational sketching are welcome, but the possible use of these conversations in the research study will be discussed and negotiated with participants on a case-by-case basis.
However, I will arrange a small conversation before and after the event with workshop/event leaders, as they can give more insights of the activity (objectives, goals, impressions, comparisons with other iterations) and help contextualise it. These conversations will be recorded and used in the study following a previous agreement.

Are there other initiatives that will take part in the research?

The research focuses on sustainable craft programmes in Devon and the Southwest. This study will be carried in several craft events and workshops in the area that have a sustainability approach.

You agree for me to join you during your event/workshop. Can participants opt out?

Participation is voluntary. If someone participating in your event does not want to be involved in the study, they can communicate it orally at the beginning of, during, and at the end of the observational activity: the person will not be observed, meaning that I will not sketch/draw them nor collect any data about them. Participants who initially agreed to take part can withdraw their participation in the middle, at the end, or up to 20 days after the event by verbally communicate they withdrawal or by sending an email with their identification data to a.fasoli@kingston.ac.uk: any sketches and drawings in which they could be identified will not be used in the study; and any personal data collected concerning them will be destroyed and/or deleted.
If you are a workshop/event leader and want to withdraw from the study, this decision must be communicated before the event takes place. If I already participated in the event, you can contact me via email or phone within 20 days to anonymise your event, and our before and after conversations will be excluded from the study. All identifiable drawings of you will not be used in the study. You can find more information on anonymity and confidentiality in the 'Data' section of this document.
Group sketches and drawing or stylised sketches (figure 5) with no quotes or references to specific participants are conceptual, therefore I reserves the right to use them if I deems them valuable for the study. This applies to all conceptual sketches whose primary function is a general representation of a collective occurrence.

Will I take photos, videos, or recording during the event?

I might take contextual photos of my sketchbook in the field (figure 4)following a previous agreement with the event leader(s) and participants. I might (or ask someone to) take pictures of myself during the act of drawing These are documentation photos that are meant to show the methodology in action. This study does not comprise other types of documentary visuals (portrait photos, videos) beyond drawings.
Previous consent by the participant, I might record casual conversations between participants, and between them and me. The audio file will accompany the drawings in the research analysis and showcase.

What data will be collected?

Sensitive data collected vary depending on your role in the research:

If you are an event leader or organiser:

In this case, the necessary data collected about you are:

  • full name
  • profession / role in the organisation

The following data are collected for statistical purposed in the context of the research, like to verify demographic trends of the people involved in craft initiatives:

  • background/education
  • ethnicity
  • age

About confidentiality and anonymity

Names, dates, and descriptions about public events and workshop are considered public information, which cannot be anonymised. If promotional material on the event has your name as the event leader/facilitator, anonymity and confidentiality regarding your role cannot be granted.
However, you can choose to participate anonymously in regard to the conversation we have before and after the event. That means that the information of those conversations will be delinked from the workshop and all identifiable information about you will be removed from the dataset of the research. Background/education, ethnicity, and age will be delinked from your name and presented only in their aggregated form.
Confidentiality is granted both during our conversations and during the event/workshop, meaning any information deemed as confidential that does not have explicit permission for disclosure will not be divulged.

If you are a participant in the event I am joining:

In this case, the following data might be collected:

  • name (Optional: you can choose to stay anonymous)

The following data are collected for statistical purposed in the context of the research, like to verify demographic trends of the people involved in craft initiatives:

  • age
  • ethnicity
  • gender
  • background/education
  • profession

About confidentiality and anonymity

You can decide whether to participate anonymously or not. Giving your full name and/or email address is optional and I will use it only for direct communication with you, if you want to receive updates on the research project. You can also decide to give just your first name. Your full name and identifiable data are confidential, and will not be disclosed unless you give your explicit permission.

How are your data processed?

All sensitive data are treated as confidential and will be processed for the purpose of the research. Data collected in this study will be processed according to the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018.
At the end of the research, research data will be appropriately archived and retained for minimum 10 years, at the end of which continued retention will be reviewed.

What will happen to the drawings, notes, and audio files and the data generated by this study?

Drawings, notes, and audio files produced are part of an online collection/showcase that aims to illustrate the journey and findings of the PhD. Sketches and drawing can be presented as standalone pictures or paired with quotes through audio files and texts.
The online showcase is built along the study, and its latest release can be found at https://sustainabilityinthemaking.com. There you can find more information about the online showcase as a research tool for this project.

As part of a PhD, data and information generated by this study will be analysed and discussed in the final dissertation, the primary output of the research. Datasets that will accompany the final dissertation will be aggregated data containing only the relevant information used in the research. Sensitive and confidential data will not be included in the dataset.

Finally, drawings, audiofiles and data analysis can be presented in conferences and public talks, in person and online, in the form of digital presentations, installations, or videos. They can be part of exhibitions in galleries and public venues, and they can be published and printed in academic and non-academic manuscripts, in the form of scientific articles, essays, blogs, or books.

Sharing my work with you

Event leaders and/or organisers will receives high resolution scans of post-produced sketches and drawings done during their event, with the possibility of sharing and using them on your own or on the organisation's social media profile, under the Creative Commons license Attribution-NonCommercial-NoDerivatives 4.0 International (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-nd/4.0/?ref=chooser-v1)
If you are a participant of the event and I made a particular drawing of you (like in figure 1) and you want to receive a high resolution scan of it, please specify it in the Informed Consent.

You will also be sent the audio recordings that involves yourself (only the ones directly involving you, you won't be sent, under any circumstances, recording involving other participants).

All files are sent via email. If the size of the file is too big, I'll share with you a safe download link and instructions via email.
